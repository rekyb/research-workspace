# Synthesis: Indonesian Teacher EdTech Onboarding & Activation Literature

## Overview

**Goal.** Gather, synthesize, and evaluate empirical research papers, peer-reviewed articles, and industry reports on three core pillars to ground our Android app growth strategy: (1) deferred vs. upfront registration, (2) loss aversion & the endowment effect in onboarding, and (3) Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) and PD credential adoption among Indonesian teachers (`Guru di Indonesia`). This serves as direct input to our Android app onboarding and activation architecture.

**Sources & literature studied (2026-07-16).** Nine peer-reviewed empirical studies, national evaluations, and industry benchmarks across three domain clusters: **TAM & PMM Adoption** (Megawati et al. 2024; Kurniawati 2025; Utomo & Lestari 2025; Setyaningrum & Susanto 2024), **Loss Aversion & Endowment Effect** (Litovsky et al. 2022 PNAS; Patel et al. 2021 Games for Health Journal; Silva et al. 2025), and **Mobile App Onboarding & Activation Benchmarks** (AppsFlyer/UXCam/CXL 2024–2026). See `sources.md` for complete verified evidence notes.

**Headline takeaways.**
1. **Upfront registration walls depress mobile activation:** Industry mobile-onboarding benchmarks (AppsFlyer/UXCam/CXL, global consumer apps) associate mandatory email/password signup before demonstrated value with a 25% to 40% immediate session drop-off. These are directional cross-industry figures, not yet measured on an Indonesian teacher population.
2. **Loss-framing plausibly lifts conversion (direction, not magnitude):** Once users create an initial teaching artifact they develop psychological ownership, so framing registration as *protecting earned progress* is directionally favored by loss-aversion theory. The lab loss-aversion ratio ($\lambda \approx 2.2$) is illustrative only and actively contested (2024 meta-analysis $\lambda \approx 1.955$, high heterogeneity), not a transferable conversion multiplier.
3. **Indonesian teachers require mobile simplicity and credential recognition:** Perceived Ease of Use (PEOU) predicts PMM adoption among Indonesian teachers ($p < 0.01$), though in a *mandatory*-use civil-service context (adoption intention/satisfaction rather than voluntary funnel choice). Completion rates increase when module completion is tied to verified professional development certificates (`Sertifikasi` / `Jam Pelatihan`) that carry official recognition.

## Feature 1: Deferred "Try-First" Registration (Zero Upfront Wall)

**Short description.** Removing account creation gates from the initial app launch screen and allowing users to immediately experience core product value before being asked to register.

**Key findings.** Industry mobile-onboarding benchmarks (AppsFlyer/UXCam/CXL 2024–2026; logged in `sources.md`) associate mandatory upfront registration with an initial session drop-off between **25% and 40%**. These are directional cross-industry consumer figures (gaming, e-commerce, SaaS), not measured on an Indonesian teacher population, and are used here to establish direction rather than a target. On low-end Android handsets common in Indonesian school districts, complex multi-field email forms and mandatory government single sign-on (`belajar.id`) create severe access friction. That friction is structural for a growing segment: under **KepmenPANRB No. 16/2025**, new honorary teachers (`Guru Honorer`) entering service after 31 December 2024 can no longer be added to Dapodik, so they never receive the `belajar.id` credential a login wall would demand — a regulation-grounded access-exclusion argument for deferring registration that is stronger than generic friction. Megawati et al. (2024, DOI: `10.19109/intelektualita.v13i1.21095`) establish that Perceived Ease of Use (PEOU) predicts PMM adoption among Indonesian junior high (`SMP`) teachers ($p < 0.01$); note this is a *mandatory*-use TAM survey measuring adoption intention/satisfaction rather than session-level funnel behavior, so it grounds the PEOU barrier, not a specific retention lift. Removing initial login walls directly mitigates that barrier. To capture registration at the moment of experienced value without re-introducing password fatigue, the app should transition from guest exploration to account creation using low-friction **WhatsApp (WA) OTP or One-Click Login** — a design inference from the PEOU evidence, not itself an empirically tested conversion mechanism.

**Why this works (rationale).** Every input field presented before value is experienced imposes cognitive load and perceived risk, which triggers immediate abandonment among time-poor, low-confidence users. Deferring registration allows motivation to accrue through enactive mastery experiences during a guest session. When users experience immediate utility without entering credentials, trust is established, converting a high-friction gate into a natural progression step.

**How to validate this feature in the future.** Conduct a randomized A/B test on Android comparing two onboarding entry variants: (Variant A) upfront Google/Phone login wall versus (Variant B) guest-first immediate entry into Module 1. Measure Day-1 activation rate (percentage of users completing Module 1) and overall funnel completion to registration. Target metric: Variant B achieves at least a 25% lift in Module 1 completion without lowering eventual registration conversion.

## Feature 2: Loss-Aversion Registration Trigger at the "Endowment Moment"

**Short description.** Triggering the account registration modal directly after the user completes `Module 1` or creates an initial classroom artifact, explicitly framed around protecting earned progress rather than creating a new account.

**Key findings.** Litovsky, Loewenstein, Horn & Olivola (2022, *PNAS* 119(34):e2202700119, DOI: `10.1073/pnas.2202700119`; logged in `sources.md`) show an **endowment effect** in which avoiding the loss of an acquired noninstrumental asset is valued more highly than acquiring an equivalent new one. The frequently cited loss-aversion ratio ($\lambda \approx 2.2$) is illustrative only: it derives from controlled (largely WEIRD-sample) lab tasks and is actively contested — a 2024 meta-analysis of 607 estimates puts $\lambda \approx 1.955$ with large heterogeneity, and a 2025 re-meta-analysis challenges its robustness — so it indicates *direction* (loss > gain), not a transferable magnitude. The general SDT principle that informational, autonomy-supportive framing outperforms controlling framing is well established (Deci & Ryan; Silva et al. 2025, DOI: `10.28945/5549`), so the loss-framing *direction* is literature-consistent. It is this study's own untested design hypothesis — not an established finding — that (a) a passive `Module 1` quiz generates weaker ownership than tangible classroom labor and (b) generating a labor-saving **`Modul Ajar / RPP` (Rencana Pelaksanaan Pembelajaran)** is therefore the better endowment object; no cited source establishes that substitution. Under this hypothesis, triggering registration after artifact generation with informational asset-protection framing ("`Simpan Modul Ajar / RPP Anda agar tidak hilang & klaim e-sertifikat kompetensi Anda`") applies loss aversion to a genuine labor investment without controlling or surveillance framing.

**Why this works (rationale).** When an Indonesian teacher finishes `Module 1`, they have invested time and cognitive effort, creating an endowment effect around their progress. If the app prompts *"Create an account to use the app"*, it feels like an administrative burden. However, when the prompt is reframed as *"Simpan progres Modul 1 Anda agar tidak hilang & klaim e-sertifikat Anda"* (Save your Module 1 progress so it isn't lost & claim your e-certificate), loss aversion is activated. The user registers to avoid forfeiting what they have already earned.

**How to validate this feature in the future.** Run an unmoderated usability and conversion experiment comparing two post-Module 1 registration modals: (Prompt 1, Gain-Framed) *"Daftar akun baru untuk mengakses Modul 2"* versus (Prompt 2, Loss-Framed) *"Simpan progres Modul 1 Anda sekarang agar tidak hilang & klaim sertifikat kompetensi Anda."* Measure conversion rate from modal presentation to completed account registration. Target metric: Prompt 2 yields a statistically significant higher registration conversion rate (an illustrative pilot target of roughly $\geq 18\%$ is a placeholder, to be replaced by a powered effect-size estimate — it is not an evidence-derived figure).

## Feature 3: Context-Anchored Intake & Competence-Recognized Credentialing

**Short description.** A lightweight, icon-first context selector (`Jenjang` / Subject) that routes teachers into short 5–15 minute asynchronous modules tied directly to recognized professional development (`PD`) competency certificates.

**Key findings.** Kurniawati (2025, DOI: `10.32550/wdstp587`; logged in `sources.md`) confirms that infrastructure gaps across 38 Indonesian provinces require digital training to be delivered in mobile-optimized asynchronous modules (~5 to 15 minutes). While Utomo & Lestari (2025) and Setyaningrum & Susanto (2024, DOI: `10.53008/kalbisocio.v11i2.4359`) demonstrate that verifiable certificates (`Sertifikasi` or `Jam Pelatihan`) drive teacher adoption — the latter grounded in a single Bekasi (`SMP`) district and not yet generalizable to outer-island (`3T`) contexts — standalone unaccredited app PDF certificates carry zero official *Angka Kredit* valuation under Indonesia's teacher-performance framework (**Perdirjen GTK No. 7607/2023**, which governs RHK / `e-Kinerja` and the 32-point-per-semester `Pengembangan Kompetensi` requirement). The immediately shippable response is tier (1): immediate exportable **`Modul Ajar / Bukti Karya`** outputs that solve daily classroom preparation needs and can be entered into a teacher's own RHK. A second tier — formal **MGMP/KKG (Musyawarah Guru Mata Pelajaran / Kelompok Kerja Guru) 32 JP** co-certification intended to carry `e-Kinerja BKN` recognition — is an unverified governance hypothesis, not a resolved design (see `## Gaps & caveats`): such credit flows through the teacher's own RHK entry approved by the `Kepala Sekolah`, and MGMP/KKG chapters are locally autonomous, subject- and district-specific volunteer bodies outside product control.

**Why this works (rationale).** Indonesian educators operate within a structured civil service and school governance framework where professional credentials (`sertifikat pelatihan`) hold high tangible and social currency. Segmenting onboarding by exact teaching level (`Jenjang: PAUD/SD/SMP/SMA/SMK`) ensures immediate cultural relevance and low cognitive overhead. Combining short concept-to-exercise loops with a downloadable competency certificate satisfies both the infrastructure realities of low-bandwidth Android devices and the extrinsic career motivation of Indonesian teachers.

**How to validate this feature in the future.** Conduct qualitative user interviews with 8–10 Indonesian public and private school teachers across urban and semi-urban districts to evaluate candidate certificate framings (`Sertifikat Kompetensi Pengajaran AI` vs. `Jam Pelatihan Mandiri`). In parallel, instrument telemetry on low-end Android test devices (3G/4G simulated networks) to verify that the icon-first intake and Module 1 complete within under 15 minutes with zero network timeouts.

## Gaps & caveats

- **Desk-literature study, not first-hand capture:** The canonical, committed evidence log is `sources.md` (a DOI table), with supplementary thematic notes kept under `data/*/notes.md` as untracked working notes (per workspace rules, non-platform literature studies use `data/` instead of `platforms/`). There are no browser/UI captures: all empirical insight into PMM's module-completion and certificate-claiming behaviour is derived from academic evaluations (Megawati et al. 2024; Kurniawati 2025; Utomo & Lestari 2025), not direct captures. No cited source studies a third-party Android app; PMM adoption research is used as the closest available proxy.
- **Deferred-registration drop-off figures are cross-industry, not teacher-specific (Feature 1):** The 25–40% upfront-wall drop-off is a global consumer-app aggregate (gaming / e-commerce / SaaS). It motivates *direction* only; the education/Indonesia/teacher-specific magnitude is unmeasured. The regulation-grounded access argument (**KepmenPANRB 16/2025** Dapodik lock for post-2024 `Guru Honorer`) is the stronger anchor. TAM/PEOU evidence comes from *mandatory*-use PMM (satisfaction), so borrowing it to justify a voluntary consumer-app choice is a directional analogy, not direct evidence.
- **Experimental laboratory vs. classroom environment:** Loss-aversion parameters ($\lambda \approx 2.2$, Litovsky et al. 2022; Patel et al. 2021) were derived in controlled behavioural and gamification trials and are contested as a stable constant (2024 meta-analysis $\lambda \approx 1.955$, high heterogeneity; 2025 robustness challenge). Treat $\lambda$ as directional only; field validation within Indonesian teacher cohorts is required before assuming any numerical lift. The quiz→`Modul Ajar / RPP` endowment substitution is this study's own untested design hypothesis, not a cited finding.
- **MGMP/KKG co-certification is an unverified governance hypothesis (Feature 3):** The proposal that app-driven **MGMP/KKG 32 JP** activity could carry official `e-Kinerja BKN` / *Angka Kredit* recognition is not established by any cited source. Under Perdirjen GTK 7607/2023, such credit flows through the teacher's own RHK entry approved by the `Kepala Sekolah`, and MGMP/KKG chapters are locally autonomous, subject- and district-specific volunteer bodies. Realizing this pathway is a multi-year, district-by-district partnerships and policy workstream (requiring validation with MGMP/KKG coordinators, Dinas Pendidikan, and BKN) — not a shippable product feature. Only tier-1 `Bukti Karya` export is treated as buildable now.
- **Self-reported TAM variances:** Several cited Indonesian studies (Setyaningrum & Susanto 2024; Utomo & Lestari 2025) rely on self-reported survey questionnaires across specific regional districts (e.g., Bekasi, Bali). Regional variations between urban Java schools and remote outer-island districts (`3T` areas) may alter the relative weight of internet infrastructure barriers versus perceived ease of use.
