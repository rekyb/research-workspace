# References — external validation of SYNTHESIS rationales

Evidence base assembled during the Principal Researcher QA pass (2026-07-15) to
corroborate or challenge each feature's "why this feature works" rationale and its
learning/efficacy claims. Sources are peer-reviewed papers, meta-analyses, or
reputable scholarly reviews retrieved via web search. Each entry lists author + year,
title, venue, a one-line finding, a working URL, and which feature it supports (S) or
challenges (C).

Scope note: these sources validate the *reasoning* behind claims already grounded in
captured evidence. They do not add new product findings and do not paper over the
thin/second-hand captures flagged in `SYNTHESIS.md` (Gaps & caveats).

## Feature 1 — Form-first generation ("no blank box") / activation & self-efficacy

- **Bandura, A. (1977). "Self-efficacy: Toward a unifying theory of behavioral
  change." *Psychological Review* 84(2), 191–215.** Foundational: of the four sources
  of self-efficacy, *enactive mastery experience* (a direct, successful performance) is
  the most potent. Every subsequent meta-analysis has replicated this ranking.
  Overview retrieved: https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/social-sciences/sources-of-self-efficacy
  — **S (F1):** a guaranteed first success is the strongest lever on a low-confidence
  teacher's belief they can use AI again.
- **Reijnen, E. et al. (2023). "Enactive mastery experience improves attitudes towards
  digital technology via self-efficacy: a pre-registered quasi-experiment."
  *Behaviour & Information Technology*.**
  https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/0144929X.2022.2162436
  — **S (F1):** actively performing the behaviour with a new technology (an enactive
  mastery experience) improves attitudes toward it via raised self-efficacy. Directly
  supports "let the teacher succeed on their own material first."

## Feature 2 — Fluency ladder: shield → minimize → teach / scaffolding & fading

- **van de Pol, J., Volman, M. & Beishuizen, J. (2010). "Scaffolding in
  Teacher–Student Interaction: A Decade of Research." *Educational Psychology Review*
  22, 271–296.** https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10648-010-9127-6
  — **S (F2):** effective scaffolding requires three features — contingency, *fading*
  (help is gradually withdrawn), and *transfer of responsibility* (the learner ends up
  doing it alone). This is precisely the shield → minimize → teach progression.
- **Lazonder, A. W. & Harmsen, R. (2016). "Meta-Analysis of Inquiry-Based Learning:
  Effects of Guidance." *Review of Educational Research* 86(3), 681–718.**
  https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.3102/0034654315627366
  — **S (F2, F3):** guidance produces a facilitative effect on learning outcomes
  (d = 0.50). Removing guidance too early hurts; withdrawing it as competence grows is
  the correct shape.

## Feature 3 — Socratic, answer-withholding tutor / guided discovery

- **Alfieri, L., Brooks, P. J., Aldrich, N. J. & Tenenbaum, H. R. (2011). "Does
  discovery-based instruction enhance learning? A meta-analysis." *Journal of
  Educational Psychology* 103(1), 1–18.**
  http://physics.uwyo.edu/~rudim/S14_JEduPsych_DoesDiscoveryLrngEnhance.pdf
  — **S and C (F3):** *guided* discovery improves outcomes (d = 0.50), but *unguided*
  discovery performs worse than explicit instruction (d = -0.38), especially in
  math/science/problem-solving. Withholding the answer helps only when it is paired with
  active guidance; simply withholding without scaffolding can harm learning. This
  refines the F3 claim (see inline flag).

## Feature 4 — Ethics sequenced after the first win / adult-learning relevance & trust

- **Knowles, M. (andragogy). Summarised in "Malcolm Knowles' Theory of Andragogy,"
  *IJRAR* (2019).** https://ijrar.org/papers/IJRAR19K9694.pdf
  — **S (F4):** adults are relevancy-oriented and problem-centred; as a learner matures,
  orientation shifts from postponed to *immediate* application. Supports making ethics
  concrete after a lived, hands-on experience rather than front-loading it abstractly.
  (Theoretical framework, not an RCT on ethics-sequencing specifically.)
- **"Psychological safety and trust as drivers of teachers' continued use of AI tools
  in classrooms." *Scientific Reports* (2025).**
  https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-13789-4
  — **S (F4, F5):** trust and psychological safety significantly enhance long-term AI
  tool use; anxiety is a major barrier. Supports the "ambient trust cues lower the
  adoption barrier" half of F4.

## Feature 5 — Bounded student surface with oversight ("Rooms") / teacher adoption

- **Scherer, R., Siddiq, F. & Tondeur, J. (2019). "The technology acceptance model
  (TAM): A meta-analytic structural equation modeling approach to explaining teachers'
  adoption of digital technology in education." *Computers & Education* 128, 13–35.**
  https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0360131518302458
  — **S (F5):** perceived usefulness, perceived ease of use, and *facilitating
  conditions* drive teachers' adoption. A bounded, observable surface that gives control
  and a defensible answer to administrators maps to these adoption predictors.
- **Scientific Reports (2025), as above.** — **S (F5):** trust as an adoption
  prerequisite is corroborated.

## Feature 7 — Concept → exercise → reflection loop / cognitive load & retrieval

- **Van Gog, T. & Paas, F. / Sweller (2010). "Cognitive Load Theory: Advances in
  Research on Worked Examples, Animations, and Cognitive Load Measurement."
  *Educational Psychology Review* 22, 375–412.**
  https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10648-010-9145-4
  — **S (F7):** for novices, worked examples and structured guidance reduce extraneous
  load and build schemas better than unguided problem-solving. Supports short,
  low-load concept-then-practice loops for low-confidence adults.
- **Roediger, H. L. & Karpicke, J. D. (2006). "Test-Enhanced Learning: Taking Memory
  Tests Improves Long-Term Retention." *Psychological Science* 17(3), 249–255.**
  https://www.researchgate.net/publication/7270829_Test-Enhanced_Learning_Taking_Memory_Tests_Improves_Long-Term_Retention
  — **S (F7):** active retrieval (an immediate exercise) beats re-reading for retention
  (replicated; Rowland 2014 meta-analysis g ≈ 0.50). Supports the "immediate exercise"
  in the loop.
  - **C (F7 — spacing):** the retrieval and cognitive-load literature supports the loop,
    but neither supports *spacing* unless the units are revisited over time. As described,
    the concept→exercise loop is massed, not distributed. The "spacing" citation is not
    earned by the design as drawn (see inline flag).

## Feature 8 — Indonesian context: PD credential + gamified onboarding

- **Deci, E. L., Koestner, R. & Ryan, R. M. (1999). "A Meta-Analytic Review of
  Experiments Examining the Effects of Extrinsic Rewards on Intrinsic Motivation."
  *Psychological Bulletin* 125(6), 627–668.**
  https://home.ubalt.edu/tmitch/642/articles%20syllabus/Deci%20Koestner%20Ryan%20meta%20IM%20psy%20bull%2099.pdf
  — **C (F8):** tangible, expected, performance/completion-contingent rewards
  significantly *undermine* free-choice intrinsic motivation (d ≈ -0.28 to -0.40). A
  certificate can reliably drive *completion* (extrinsic) but is not a "proven driver"
  of durable interest, and can erode intrinsic motivation if it feels controlling.
  Self-Determination Theory's nuance: rewards framed as *competence feedback*
  (informational) are far less corrosive than controlling ones. Reframe the certificate
  as recognition/competence signal, not a bribe (see inline flag).
- **Sailer, M. et al. / "Gamification enhances student intrinsic motivation, perceptions
  of autonomy and relatedness, but minimal impact on competency: a meta-analysis and
  systematic review." *Educational Technology Research & Development* (2023).**
  https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11423-023-10337-7
  — **S and C (F8):** gamification lifts motivation/engagement (supports the Ruangguru
  observation) but has *minimal* impact on competency; points/badges shift focus to the
  reward rather than the learning, and shallow application underperforms. Corroborates
  the synthesis's own caveat: reward *return/consistency*, not shallow speed.
- **"Evaluating the acceptance and usability of a Kiswahili-localised mobile phone app
  in Kenya: a case of the M-Pesa app." (2024).**
  https://www.researchgate.net/publication/392822287
  — **S (F8):** localising a mobile app into the user's language improves acceptance and
  usability, corroborating the Bahasa-first / jenjang-anchored onboarding argument.
  (Industry surveys also report ~75% of users prefer software in their native language;
  cited as supporting context, not a peer-reviewed finding.)
