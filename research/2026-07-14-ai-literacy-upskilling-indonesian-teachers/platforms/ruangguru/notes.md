# Ruangguru: Observations & Patterns

**Platform type:** Indonesia's largest private **edtech** (tutoring / learning app); Bahasa-first,
mobile-first, consumer.
**Tier:** Secondary, **context anchor for RQ7 (local UX expectations) and RQ2 (engagement/
gamification mechanics)**.
**Capture method:** Public marketing landing captured first-hand (`screenshots/`); in-app learning
is mostly **paid** (guardrail: not purchased), so engagement-mechanic detail is externally cited
(*external*). **Date:** 2026-07-15.

---

## Why it's in the study
Ruangguru shapes what Indonesian *students* (and their teachers) expect a learning app to feel
like: Bahasa, mobile, colourful, gamified, level-segmented. It's the consumer-UX baseline our
design will be judged against, even though it targets students, not teacher PD.

## RQ7 (context-fit): localized, level-segmented onboarding
First-hand: the landing immediately opens a Bahasa onboarding modal, **"Selamat datang di
Ruangguru! Ingin tahu produk untuk jenjang apa?"** (which level?) offering **PAUD / SD / SMP / SMA
/ UTBK-SNBT**, then **"Pilih kelas"** (Kelas 10/11/12) (`01-bahasa-onboarding-modal.png`). Takeaways:
- **Segment-by-level on first contact.** The app orients around the Indonesian schooling structure
  (jenjang/kelas) from the very first screen. Our onboarding should likewise anchor to the
  teacher's real context (jenjang, mapel/subject) immediately.
- **Bahasa-first, warm, promo-driven.** Heavy discounting ("Diskon 52%", "Festival") signals a
  price-sensitive consumer market, reinforcing that anything we ask teachers to *pay* for will
  meet resistance (supports free + PD-recognized framing).

## RQ2 (engagement / gamification): the mechanic teachers/students already know (⟶ external)
- **"Starchamps" gamification:** students earn **points for accurate and fast quiz answers**, with
  **badges** and healthy competition; combined with AI-personalized learning paths this drove a
  reported **~30% increase in engagement in 2024** (⟶ external). Points/badges/leaderboard-style
  competition is the familiar engagement grammar here.
- **Implication:** if we add habit/engagement mechanics (which MagicSchool/Khanmigo lack), a
  points-and-streaks model is culturally legible to Indonesian users because Ruangguru normalized
  it. But note the caution from other studies: reward-for-speed can undermine deep learning, use
  gamification to drive *return/consistency*, not to reward shallow fast answers.

## Cross-cutting note
Ruangguru also runs **Ruangkerja** (corporate upskilling) and Brain/English Academy, and positions
"Learning Center" tools for teachers to manage class participation and monitor performance
(⟶ external), so the company already blends consumer gamification with teacher-facing classroom
management, a model of combining both.

## Gaps / caveats
- In-app learning + gamification are **paid / login-gated**; not captured first-hand. The landing
  onboarding is first-hand; mechanic detail is externally cited.
- **Student-focused, not teacher-PD.** It informs *localized UX and engagement expectations*, not
  AI-literacy content or the teacher-fluency strand.
- No AI-literacy offering observed; relevance is UX/engagement context only.
