# Coursera: observations & patterns

**Role in this study:** stands in for **"paid credential framing"** (a generic MOOC, not
teacher-PD-specific). Captured logged-out on *Learning How to Learn*.

## What stood out
- **Certificate = shareable credential, not a trophy.** The single most prominent certificate
  cue is "Shareable certificate → Add to your LinkedIn profile" with a LinkedIn logo
  (`02-shareable-certificate.png`). Coursera sells the certificate on its *external signalling
  value*, not on the satisfaction of completion. This maps directly to the literature: adult
  learners pursue a certificate only when they see a concrete professional use
  (Perdue 2023), and credential *visibility* is what drives labour-market payoff
  (Athey & Palikot 2024). See `references.md`.
- **The reward is teased before any effort.** The certificate framing is on the course
  landing page, visible before enrolment, priming the learner with the payoff up front.
- **Gated by account + payment.** Clicking "Enroll now" produces an auth wall
  (`03-auth-gate.png`); the certificate proper is paid. This is the credential-as-paid-product
  model: the learning can be audited free, the *credential* costs money.

## Pattern worth synthesizing
- **"Credential you can show" framing.** When the goal is extrinsic (finish + get recognised),
  lead with where the credential is displayed and to whom (LinkedIn / employer), not with the
  act of completing. Contrast with Duolingo, where the reward is intrinsic/in-app progress.

## Gaps / caveats
- Post-purchase certificate artifact **not captured** (paywall + account; guardrails). The
  observable evidence stops at the framing and the auth gate.
- Coursera is not teacher-specific; treat it as the "paid credential" reference point, with
  Google Teacher Center as the teacher-PD counterpart.
